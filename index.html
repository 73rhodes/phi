De Divina Proportione

Van de Graaf's 'Secret Canon' for single-column broadsheet. The text area is not in proportion to the page, because the back margins which normally form a central gutter dividing verso & recto facing pages are incorporated into the main text area. The left foredge, top and foot margins end up being the same as verso/recto book layout, however.

Van de Graaf's canon works on pages of any proportion, which is well-suited to scrolling media like the web. Although they arrived at the construction independently, Van de Graaf's and Raúl Rosarivo's canons are identical, being based on a division of the page into ninths. Van de Graaf arrived at his canon by utilising the technique of Villard de Honnecourt

Manually set, intentionally unbalanced verso/recto layout using 'row' and 'page' classes. The outer margins are established by the vandegraaf class but the back margins are set by the "page" class. I'd prefer to have margins integral to the page, but the problem is, floated divs with differing heights have their margins (padding actually) calculated according to their own height and not the height of the encompassing vandegraaf "book".

This should be useful for creating a text column with illustrations, etc. in the adjacent column, but with whitespace between them. Not sure if there is a better way to do it; this works for now. Tschichold's "Golden Canon" adapted to a single-column, variable-height broadsheet layout with margins in 3/4/6 proportion. The text area does not conform to the Golden Section because the height is indeterminate, but Tschichold seemed to be as concerned with the proportions of the page margins as with the proportions of the text area.

The resulting modified Tschichold canon can be used with a Golden Rectangle containing an image, for example, to reproduce the proportions of his original construction. The image below is cropped in the Golden Section with margins in the proportion 3/4/6.

  • Villard de Honnecourt and Euclidian Geometry, Marie-Therese Zenner, http://www.emis.de/journals/NNJ/Zenner.html () This article tries to draw some connections between Euclid's geometry and the sketches of Villard de Honnecourt, proposing that the technique of dividing a space into 1/2, 1/3, etc can be seen in his work. It doesn't go so far as attribution. The so-called "Villard Diagram" seems to be an invention of Daniele Capo, who constructed it and made the attribution to Villard, however Tenuous. It does seem likely, however, that Villard and other architects and artists probably from Phidias (490-430 BC - who built the Parthenon statues)... from the ancient Greeks anyway... and certainly from Euclid (325-265 BC) onwards, knew methods of geometric construction that are illustrated by the so-called Villard Diagram. And not only the technique of dividing spaces into Nths, but of creating equilateral triangles and a great many other ingenious techniques for architecture and building. What the article by Marie-Therese Zenner is particularly interesting for is it's assertion that from the time of the ancient Greeks through the renaissance, from Euclid to Vitruvius to (Villard) de Honnecourt to da Vinci, artist and architect were two aspects of the same profession, and a knowledge of geometry was absolutely a requirement whether it was artistic rendering or engineering. You can see Honnecourts complete folio at https://archive.org/details/albumdevillardde00vill - it does not contain the so-called Villard Diagram. It certainly doesn't use any of these canons of page construction to define the layout of his sketchbook - as one tumblr post claimed - the margins are completely inconsistent, accidental, and frequently non-existent!
  • http://www.ac-grenoble.fr/argouges/v1/PEDAGOGI/MAREAU/feuille_de_papier.htm Seems to be the main source of attribution for the so-called Villard Diagram. It mentions Honnecourt but contains no supporting evidence that the technique of dividing a space into N parts came from him. It seems likely that this technique was known at least since Euclid and used by many artist-architects from ancient Greece onwards. This is the most definitive source I could find for the so-called "Villard Diagram" and claim that it is used for page construction. It describes a "Trace de Villard de Honnecourt". I suspect that this claim and attribution could be motivated in part by wanting to attribute the canon of page construction to a French architect. Nothing in Honnecourt's folio or his writing actually relates to page layout - as the folio itself attests.
